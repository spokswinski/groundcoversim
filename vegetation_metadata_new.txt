ABSTRACT:				
Longleaf pine ecosystems are rich in plant species and represent the dominant upland forest type in several southeastern military bases. In order to manage these forests to fulfill military objectives and to conserve this unique natural resource, we investigated mechanisms of diversity and neutral theory. We coupled a series of field experiments with data mining exercises to help managers meet their objectives for monitoring the impact of various activities on the understory plant community. The data collected from these experiments will then go towards the development of modeling tools to help evaluate different management techniques based on links between overstory structure, fire and understory plant diversity. Vegetation sampling techniques and data are critical components to link forest structure measured by remote sensing to interaction diversity and to evaluate the neutral theory of biological diversity, and fire as random mortality agent, as a possible mechanisms for high diversity in areas of with frequent low-intensity fires.  Research took place on Eglin AFB, FL from fall 2012 through fall 2016. All groundcover monitoring plots were burned during the springs of 2013 and 2015, and monitored every fall from 2012 to 2016. 			
				
METHODS: 				
Plot selection:	Monitoring plot locations were co-located within Jackson Guard monitoring plots (established in 2003-2006) listed as high quality sandhills and flatwoods habitat that were scheduled to burn in fiscal year 2012 (contact: Brett Williams, Wildland Fire Manager at Jackson Guard). Using a stem map of the overstory in the Jackson Guard monitoring plots, all groundcover monitoring plots were placed outside a 4 m buffer from any overstory tree. 
			

GROUNDCOVER MONITORING PLOTS: 
Groundcover monitoring plots were 1m X 3m in size, oriented longwise north to south. Three plots were distributed within five sandhill burn units and five flatwoods burn units for a total of 30 groundcover monitoring plots. Each quadrat or 1m2 area within the plots were monitored at a 100 cm2 resolution using a 1m2 square frame. The square frame was a rigid aluminum frame with inside dimensions of 1m X 1m. Along the upper edge of the square, grooves were cut every ten centimeters so that 3 mm stainless steel rods were placed in a grid pattern. The outside portion of the square had four vertical sleeves at the corners that were lined up with two inside planes of the square. When establishing each quadrat, four one inch stainless steel rods were driven through the sleeves. The square frame was placed on two of the rods, then shifted twice across the 1m x 3m area to create a 10 x 300 cell grid with a 100 cm2 resolution. Monitoring began in the lower southwest corner of the quadrat moving east through the row and then to the next row north. Presence was recorded for each species found in an individual cell.

Data entry and QA/QC:	Data was entered into Microsoft Excel from hardcopies of recorded data, each on a separate worksheet. The worksheets were compared on a third worksheet and hardcopies were referenced for changes. Once all corrections were made, a final worksheet was transposed and added to the master vegetation file. Beginning in 2013, copies of the previous years data were taken into the field to check for accuracy. Additionally, all data was copied to a separate worksheet following initial entry and species code data was sorted alphabetically. Any misspelled species codes were corrected in the master file.
